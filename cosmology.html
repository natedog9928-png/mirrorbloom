The Foundation

Cosmology

One flat world, split top and bottom like a reflection on still water.

Mirrorbloom is a single world, not two. Picture a disc, or a coin — the upper face is whimsical, beautiful, Alice-coded fantasy. The lower face is its exact dark mirror: pure horror. They are not separate planes so much as two sides of the same thing, bound by a single law that governs everything else in this setting.

The Law of Reflection

Beauty and goodness generated above directly feeds horror and evil below. It is not metaphorical and it is not optional — it is closer to a law of physics than a moral teaching. A kindness performed in Mirrorbloom is, somewhere beneath it, paid for.

Above

Fifteen empires, whimsical and Alice-coded. Joy, hope, beauty, and abundance, generated by mortal lives lived well.

Below

Fifteen corrupted twin empires — same names, same peoples, inverted. Every empire above casts an exact shadow below.

The Breaking

Before any of this, there was no above and no below — there was only the Great Hope and the Great Despair, two halves of a single radiant whole, though no living thing remembers what that whole was called, or whether it had a name at all.

Once, there was one being, whole — or two who were as one. Something happened between them that no one now remembers, or no one was ever told. The world split in the breaking: above, joy, untouched and bright. Below, despair, sharp-toothed and starving. The one above does not know what became of the one below. The one below has convinced herself something was stolen — a crown, a right, a throne — because that is easier to survive believing than the truth: that she is simply, completely alone.

The cause of the split is, even in-world, a genuine mystery — no scholar, court, or scripture agrees on why it happened, and that uncertainty is itself part of the setting's design. What's known for certain: the Great Hope does not remember what became of the Great Despair, and does not know what her shadow now believes was taken from her. The Great Despair, for her part, has built an entire identity, an entire crusade, around reclaiming a crown that never existed — because admitting what she actually lost would mean admitting she is simply, irreparably alone.

Born from the Breaking

The Six Great Fey Lords were not witnesses to the Breaking — they were born from it, the way light scatters into a spectrum when it passes through broken glass. Each Lord-and-shadow pair is a fragment of what the Great Hope and the Great Despair were, splintered off in the moment of breaking: the very instant of beginning and ending, frozen into being; creation and destruction, no longer sharing a body; joy and cruelty, divided; imagination split from nightmare; love-as-welcome torn from love-as-consumption; play, separated from itself.

Only the Fey Lords know the truth of what happened during the Breaking. The Great Hope has been sealed away from this knowledge — whether by the Lords' design, by the nature of the Breaking itself, or by something else entirely is one of the few mysteries even this document won't resolve for you.

Crossing Between Halves

Each god and each Fey Lord aspect holds a realm of their own — fourteen in total — tied closely to Mirrorbloom but standing outside it, reachable only through doorways. Some doorways are fixed and well-mapped, marked on old charts and guarded or worshipped accordingly. Others wander, opening and closing without warning, in places that held nothing the morning before.

Through these doors, a Fey Lord may pull a being out into Mirrorbloom, or push a piece of their own realm into the world — a patch of ground that simply becomes, for a time or forever, somewhere else entirely.
