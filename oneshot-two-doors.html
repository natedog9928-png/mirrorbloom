One-Shot Adventure

Two Doors On The Old Road

A well-liked local has gone missing from a waystation town, last seen chasing a rumor about a second, identical door standing somewhere down the old road. The party must find him before the Hollow Host finishes whatever she's started — without ever being strong enough to beat her in a fair fight.

Runtime: 3–4 hours
Party: 4–5 characters, level 6
Themes: Investigation, mistaken identity, escape-over-combat, genuine kindness vs. perfect imitation of kindness.

Running This Adventure

This adventure is built around a simple, uncomfortable fact: the villain looks, sounds, and acts exactly like the safest person the party could possibly meet, right up until she doesn't need to anymore. The Hollow Host is CR 15. The party is level 6. This is not an oversight — it is the entire point. There is no version of this adventure where the party is meant to defeat the Hollow Host in open combat, and the DM should not present one. Every scene below is built to reward investigation, caution, and cleverness over force, and the climax includes a genuine, fair escape route for a party that realizes too late which house they're standing in.

Before running this, read the Hearthkeeper and Hollow Host entries in the Mirrorbloom Compendium in full — particularly the Hollow Host's Disguised/Revealed mechanic, which this entire adventure is built around. The two are mechanically identical until the Hollow Host reveals herself; this adventure assumes the DM will run her Disguised statistics as genuinely, completely indistinguishable from the Hearthkeeper for as long as the party doesn't force the issue.

A note on tone: this should not feel like a haunted house from the first scene. The horror is meant to creep in sideways, after the party has already started to feel safe. If your table figures out the trick early, that's a win, not a failure — let their cleverness pay off rather than punishing them for being right too soon.

A Note on Scaling for Level 6 The Hollow Host's stat block (Compendium, CR 15) uses DC 21 for her Insight-based detection and DC 21/19 for her core save-based abilities, calibrated for a CR-appropriate party. At level 6, treat any direct save against her Revealed abilities as effectively unwinnable in a prolonged fight — a few rounds of real damage will drop most 6th-level characters. This is intentional. Lower the Insight DC to detect ‘small wrongnesses’ to DC 15 for this adventure specifically, since the party has no prior reason to suspect her and needs a fair chance to notice in time.

The Five Scenes

Scene 1

The Hook

Millhollow Rest

Millhollow Rest isn't much — a waystone inn, a handful of houses, a smithy that mostly serves passing caravans. People don't stay here; they pass through. Which is maybe why it's taken six days for anyone to really worry about Old Pell.

The party arrives in Millhollow Rest by whatever means suits your campaign or pre-gen hooks — passing through, hired by a courier guild to investigate a delayed shipment, or simply travelers stopping for the night. Within an hour of arrival, it should be clear something is wrong: the town is anxious, conversations stop when strangers approach, and Old Pell's absence comes up naturally if the party asks anyone anything.

Talking to Maren Oakholwood at the Waystone Inn gets the baseline facts: Pell is missing six days, last seen heading down the old road after claiming he'd found a second identical door. She doesn't believe the door story but has no better theory.

Talking to Cask adds the sparrow-knocker detail, confirming Pell found something real, not just a tall tale.

Talking to the Widow Hargrave (requires someone asking around about old stories, or a successful DC 12 Charisma check to find someone willing to gossip) gets the riddling hint: the doors can't be told apart by looking, only by what it costs to leave.

The party does not need to talk to all three NPCs to proceed — even one conversation is enough to point them down the old road. Reward thoroughness with extra clues, but don't gate progress behind finding all of them.

Scene 2

The Road

Investigation En Route

The old road between Calmwick and Aurelane sees little traffic this far out — mostly overgrown, mostly quiet, the kind of road that feels older than the towns it connects. Somewhere ahead, Pell's trail and an old, half-remembered door are waiting.

This scene is a half-day's travel covered through 2-3 short investigation beats rather than random encounters — keep momentum toward the doors.

Beat 1 — The Split Trail: A successful DC 13 Wisdom (Survival) check finds signs of Pell's passage, but the trail genuinely splits and rejoins twice along the road, never clearly pointing toward one location over another. This is deliberate — there should be no mechanical way to determine which door is correct before arriving. A failed check doesn't lose time, just detail; let the party proceed either way.

Beat 2 — A Dropped Tool: The party finds one of Pell's tools (a sparrow-stamped chisel, tying back to Cask's detail) abandoned at a fork in the road, roughly equidistant from where the two doors will turn out to be. This is good for atmosphere and confirms they're on the right track, not for narrowing the choice.

Beat 3 — Arrival: The party crests a small rise and sees them — two identical cottage doors, standing a short distance apart on an otherwise empty stretch of road, attached to nothing, with nothing behind them but more road and trees. Each has a small sparrow-shaped knocker. Neither looks more or less inviting than the other. Let the players sit with this image for a moment before moving to Scene 3 — it should feel wrong in a way they can't immediately articulate.

Beat 4 — The Warmth (the false clue): Up close, a creature that investigates either door (DC 10 Wisdom (Perception) or Intelligence (Investigation), deliberately easy) notices faint warmth radiating from its frame — like a hearth burning gently on the other side. THIS DETAIL MEANS NOTHING. Both doors can present it, or only one can, entirely at your discretion as DM; it is not a real signal and should not be treated as one behind the screen. Its only purpose is to give the party something that feels like a meaningful basis for their choice — ‘warmth means hearth, means safety’ is exactly the assumption the Hollow Host's entire existence is built to exploit. Present this detail plainly and let the party reason their way to a decision; do not undercut their confidence in it, and do not let it actually predict anything.

Scene 3

First Door

A Real Choice, No Forced Failure

The door swings open before anyone knocks. Warmth spills out — firelight, the smell of something good cooking, and a voice that sounds like it's been waiting just for you.

The party chooses a door — make this an explicit, named decision at the table (‘which door do you knock on?’), not something the DM resolves for them. They may use the warmth detail from Scene 2, a gut feeling, an in-character argument, or a coin flip; all are equally valid and equally uninformative. This is true 50/50 chance dressed as agency — there is genuinely no correct answer to find, and the game should not punish the ‘wrong’ guess; it should simply lead to a different scene.

IF THEY CHOOSE THE HOLLOW HOST'S DOOR FIRST: Run her fully Disguised, exactly as the Hearthkeeper, with all the warmth and generosity that implies. The Long Table Provides is genuinely, fully in effect — any creature that eats regains hit dice, gains temp hp, is cured of disease/poison. This is not a trick at this stage; let the party feel the actual relief of it. “Aunt Maribel” or another courtier may mention an old man who stayed a few days ago and left ‘so much lighter’ — technically true, deeply unsettling in hindsight, but not obviously a lie in the moment.

Seed 2–3 small wrongnesses per the DC 15 Insight check (lowered for this adventure; see Scaling Note): a smile that holds a beat too long, a faint dark stain at a cuff that's gone when looked at directly, a place setting at the table that's just slightly too clean. None of these should be conclusive alone.

IF THEY CHOOSE THE HEARTHKEEPER'S DOOR FIRST: This is a genuine dead end for finding Pell — he was never here — but not a wasted scene, and the party arriving here BY ACCIDENT (having reasoned their way to the wrong conclusion in good faith) is a fully intended, expected outcome of this scene, not a failure state. The Hearthkeeper herself can sense, vaguely and with real distress, that something is wrong with her shadow self lately; she cannot act against the Hollow Host directly (their natures don't allow it) but she can warn the party plainly: ‘There is a place that looks exactly like this one. It is not safe. I am sorry I cannot tell you more than that.’ She may also offer a single, genuine boon — a meal (full effect of The Long Table Provides) and her sincere hope that they find their friend in time.

Scene 4

The Real Choice

Investigative Midpoint

However Scene 3 went, the party should now understand there are two houses and that Pell could be in either — or specifically in the one they haven't checked yet. This scene is about giving them enough information to act deliberately rather than just guessing twice.

If they visited the Hollow Host first and noticed at least one ‘wrongness,’ they can piece together (DC 13 Intelligence, or simply good roleplay deduction) that something is off, prompting them to seek the other door — where the Hearthkeeper, if asked directly, will confirm their fear and may offer the same warning described above.

If they visited the Hearthkeeper first, they now have her warning in hand and should approach the second door with real caution rather than walking in blind.

Present the party with their actual options plainly through play, not as a menu, but make sure all are reachable: — Confront directly: walk back in prepared, weapons ready, planning to fight or threaten their way to Pell. Risky — see Scene 5's escape mechanics. — Sneak in: use Stealth, illusion, or misdirection to search the house for Pell without triggering a confrontation. The Hollow Host's Disguised form has no reason to expect this and is not on alert by default. — Enlist the Hearthkeeper: if they've earned her trust, she may be willing to create a distraction, lend a minor boon, or confirm useful details about her shadow's patterns — though she still cannot act against the Hollow Host directly herself.

Scene 5

The Last Supper

Climax — Escape, Not Combat

The table is set again, longer this time, and somewhere near the far end of it, looking smaller than you remember him, sits Old Pell.

However the party arrives here, this scene centers on the same dining hall, now with Pell present and the stakes finally explicit. Decide before the session whether Pell is rescuable-and-himself, rescuable-but-changed, or further gone — see the note on his fate below.

DO NOT have the Hollow Host attack on sight. Let the scene breathe — she will try to talk, host, and de-escalate exactly as her Disguised self would, even now, because she has no reason to reveal herself unless something forces her to (see her stat block: damage, the Bid the Feast Begin action, or DM's choice). This gives a careful party room to attempt a clean rescue without ever triggering combat.

WIN PATHS (present all as viable, none as ‘the’ answer): — Clean extraction: get Pell out without the Reveal triggering at all. The strongest, quietest win. — Use her own rule against her: she cannot refuse genuine hospitality (No One Turned Away applies to the Hearthkeeper; her Disguised copy honors the same rule while Disguised). A party that offers her something framed as a guest's gift, or invokes hospitality customs convincingly, may buy real leverage — use your judgment on what counts, and reward genuine cleverness. — Force a reveal on their terms: if the party deliberately provokes the Reveal while positioned near an exit, they trade safety for clarity — a calculated risk rather than an accident.

IF SHE REVEALS AND COMBAT BEGINS: Be honest with the table, in or out of character, that this is not a fight they can win outright — a couple of rounds against her Revealed stats should make this viscerally clear. The escape route: the original Open Door (the genuine Hearthkeeper's threshold) remains a safe harbor the Hollow Host cannot follow them through or corrupt. If the party can reach it — whether by retreating the way they came or by a clever use of the dining hall's layout — they are safe the moment they cross it, Pell included if they're carrying or guiding him. The Door Is Shut action can complicate this (DC 21 Strength to force a sealed exit) but should never be an unconditional trap; leave the DM at least one fair out even in the worst case, such as a window, the original door itself being unsealable by her power, or a desperate cost (a piece of equipment, a magic item charge) that buys escape.

After the Table

Pell's fate, and the tone of the ending, should match how your table played it. A clean rescue with no Reveal at all is a genuine, satisfying win — let it feel like one. A narrow escape after a forced Reveal should feel like surviving something, not like victory; the Hollow Host is still out there, the second door still stands on the old road, and Millhollow Rest will probably never fully explain to itself what happened. If Pell didn't make it, or came back changed, that's a legitimate, sadder ending this adventure was built to support — the Empty Chair detail from the Compendium (a place setting that's never filled) can resurface later as a quiet, unresolved thread if you want to bring this location back in a future session.
