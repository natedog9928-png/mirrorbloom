Empire I of Fifteen

The Shining Everlast & the Last Shine

The largest, most powerful empire in Mirrorbloom — and its corrupted mirror twin.

The largest, most powerful empire in Mirrorbloom — a single continuous sprawl of towers reaching for a sky they can never quite touch.

Overview

The Shining Everlast is not a city with surrounding farmland and villages; it is, in its entirety, one unbroken urban mass, home to some fifty million people across ten major species and hundreds of minor ones. Its towers, some rivaling the tallest structures any mortal civilization has ever raised, are not merely architecture — they are the empire's central act of faith, a literal, physical reaching toward the Great Hope's domain, built by a people who have never stopped believing that if they build high enough, something up there might notice.

The empire runs on Hopelight: magic drawn from a dense convergence of ley lines beneath the city, refined into crystal that powers everything from streetlamps to the vast transit lifts connecting the city's vertical tiers, and present in lesser form as a baseline magical aptitude nearly every citizen carries from birth. For generations this system has made the Shining Everlast the wealthiest, most technologically dazzling empire in Mirrorbloom — and for the first time in living memory, it is beginning to fail. Hopelight crystal forms slowly, fed by genuine, sustained collective hope, and the city's hunger for expansion has begun to outpace what new hope the population can generate. The Senate, representative body of the empire's major species, has spent three years failing to agree on what to do about it.

Government

The Shining Everlast is governed by a Senate, with seats allocated proportionally by species rather than geography. Five major factions currently dominate the chamber, each holding a fundamentally different theory of how to address the Hopelight crisis, and none commanding anything close to a working majority. The Senate is presided over by a Speaker, elected to chair rather than vote, theoretically standing above factional politics.

Geography — Eight Vertical Zones

The Underlevels
Below street level

Canals, water systems, old foundations, and the deepest Hopelight conduit infrastructure run through here, predominantly tended by Tidewalkers. The least glamorous tier and the most essential — the rest of the city would stop running within days without it. The Underlevels feel the Hopelight shortage last, since infrastructure receives rationing priority, but would be hit hardest of all tiers if the system ever truly failed.

The Ground Bazaar
Street level

Dense, chaotic, multi-species marketplaces and transit hubs, where Rabbitfolk courier networks crisscross a working-class population drawn from every major species and dozens of minor ones. This is the city's most visibly cosmopolitan tier, and the one that feels day-to-day Hopelight rationing most directly — dimming lamps, slowed transit lifts, the small daily inconveniences of a system running short.

The Foundry Tiers
Lower-mid towers

Emberforged and Orc engineering guild territory: manufacturing floors, Hopelight refineries, and the structural heart of the city's construction trade. Industrial, loud, and lit by working forges even when the rest of the city dims. The Deepwright faction's political power is strongest here, among workers proud of their craft and increasingly anxious about what they're being asked to dig for.

The Greenbelt Spires
Mid towers

Rootkin-tended vertical gardens and residential terraces, surprisingly quiet and lush for a tier hundreds of stories up. Genuine parks, orchards, and tended green space wind through this level, maintained with the patient, generational care Rootkin culture is known for. The Verdant Accord's stronghold, and the site of the city's most beloved public festivals.

The Lamplight Quarter
Mid-upper towers

Mixed residential and commercial space where most of the city's multi-species middle class actually lives. Duskborn-run nightlife and after-hours trade dominate once the sun goes down, and the district stays genuinely alive around the clock. A natural home base for visitors who need a foothold in the city without the extremes of the Underlevels or the Crownspires.

The Glass Courts
Upper towers

Senate chambers, judiciary, and the Glasswright community's heart, architecturally the most overtly magical-looking tier in the city — translucent walkways, light-refracting halls, and the chamber where Speaker Hale and the five senators conduct (or fail to conduct) the empire's business.

The Stormreach
Highest inhabited towers

Stormling territory and the domain of the city's highest-altitude tower maintenance crews, alongside the wealthiest non-institutional residential spires below the very top. Genuinely dangerous to live in if a tower destabilizes — height carries real risk here, a fact the Stormreach's wealthy residents mostly choose not to think about.

The Crownspires
The literal tower-tops

The oldest elf-built towers' peaks alongside the newest ultra-luxury construction, smallest in population by far and closest, both symbolically and literally, to the Great Hope's domain. Minor, mostly unconfirmed shrine-doors to the Unclouded are rumored to exist somewhere up here, tended by a small and very private community of true believers.

Ten Major Species

Humans

Appearance

As varied as any human population anywhere in Mirrorbloom — no single build, skin tone, or feature set dominates. What marks an Everlast-born human, more than anything physical, is posture: a habit of looking up, toward the towers, that visitors from other empires find distinctive within a generation or two of settling here.

Role

Everywhere. Senate seats, guild leadership, street vendors, tower engineers — humans fill every economic and social niche the city offers, which is precisely the point; they're the empire's connective tissue.

Elves

Appearance

Tall, long-limbed, and unmistakably patient in their movements — Everlast elves favor severe, architectural fashion that echoes the towers themselves, sharp vertical lines and minimal ornamentation. Many carry faint, old burn-scars from the early, less stable days of Hopelight refinement, worn without embarrassment as a mark of lineage.

Role

Architecture, old-guard senate seats, long-horizon magical research — elves are disproportionately represented wherever patience is the actual asset being sold.

Orcs

Appearance

Powerfully built, with the practical calluses and burn-resistant skin of generations spent in close proximity to forges and load-bearing stonework. Orc fashion in the Everlast favors functional reinforced clothing over decoration — though guild colors and clan-markings are worn proudly, often dyed directly into work leathers.

Role

Structural engineering, construction guilds, heavy industry — the literal builders of the skyline, including the Foundry Tiers' backbone workforce.

Rabbitfolk

Appearance

Small, quick, long-eared humanoids with an enormous range of fur coloration and pattern — no two rabbitfolk families look quite alike, which they consider a point of pride rather than oddity. Built for speed and tight spaces, equally at home darting through Ground Bazaar crowds or scrambling along tower exterior maintenance lines.

Role

Courier networks, transit systems, message-running between districts and altitudes — if something needs to move fast across the Shining Everlast, a rabbitfolk clan is usually involved.

Glasswrights

Appearance

Humanoids with translucent, faintly luminous skin that shifts subtly with genuine emotion — most noticeably, traces of actual Hopelight glow through when a Glasswright feels real joy, a trait no amount of practice fully suppresses.

Role

Diplomacy, judiciary, public records — anywhere institutional trust matters more than persuasive flexibility. The Glasswright Compact's entire platform stems directly from this cultural trait.

Stormlings

Appearance

Winged humanoids descended from creatures who once lived among the high clouds the towers now physically pierce; feathered or membrane-winged depending on lineage, with keen distance vision and a restless, wind-tested look about them even indoors.

Role

Tower maintenance at extreme height, message-running between the Stormreach and Crownspires, scouting and exploration — the empire's literal eyes on the horizon.

Rootkin

Appearance

Plant-adjacent humanoids with bark-textured or leaf-flecked skin, growing visibly slower than most other species — a Rootkin in their first century of life might still be physically maturing, a fact that shapes nearly everything about how their families function.

Role

Tower gardens, the city's few true green spaces, slow-cultivation magic — almost the entirety of the Greenbelt Spires' upkeep runs through Rootkin hands.

Emberforged

Appearance

Humanoids with warm, stone-and-metal-flecked skin that genuinely radiates low heat, born near (by tradition, if not always literal fact) the city's old forges. Their skin often carries faint mineral seams that catch the light, especially Hopelight, in ways other species' skin doesn't.

Role

Artisans, engineers, Hopelight infrastructure — much of the actual refinement process that turns raw crystal into usable power runs through Emberforged hands and workshops.

Tidewalkers

Appearance

Amphibious humanoids with smooth, faintly damp-looking skin and webbed extremities, equally capable below the city's canals and on its streets, though most prefer the Underlevels' humidity to the drier air above.

Role

Underlevel infrastructure, water systems, canal transit — the unglamorous, essential machinery that keeps the rest of the city's glamour running.

Duskborn

Appearance

Pale, quiet humanoids who are visibly more comfortable and alert after dark, with large, light-sensitive eyes and a calm, unhurried way of moving through crowds even at the city's busiest hours.

Role

After-hours economy, night markets, the Lamplight Quarter's entertainment district — anything the city needs running while most of it sleeps.

The Senate

Toravin Hale

Speaker of the Senate — Human

Neutral Chair

Stance: Presides over the senate without a vote, theoretically above the five factions' infighting. In practice, decades in the chair have taught him exactly how to use procedure as leverage — which motions get tabled, whose petitions get fast-tracked, which crises get an emergency session and which quietly wait their turn.

Hook: A party that earns Toravin's trust may be the only people he'd ever consider telling the truth to — and the only ones positioned to act on it without triggering the senate's collective panic.

Voss Kael

Speaker-Senator — Emberforged

The Deepwrights

Stance: Believes the Hopelight crisis is a supply problem with a supply solution: dig further into the ley convergence, accept the structural risk, push past old safety boundaries. Heavily backed by Emberforged and Orc engineering guilds who profit from expanded mining contracts.

Hook: The suppressed report still exists, filed somewhere it shouldn't be. Whoever finds it holds real leverage over the most powerful faction in the senate.

Ilyenne Wovenbark

First-Root Senator — Rootkin

The Verdant Accord

Stance: Argues the crisis isn't about mining, it's about cultivating genuine civic joy and purpose at scale — festivals, public works, reducing despair-driving poverty — since Hopelight crystal forms from sustained collective hope.

Hook: A party that can show Ilyenne hard evidence of the actual timeline might be the only ones capable of shaking her glacial patience into something resembling urgency — for better or worse.

Coris Brask

Senator-Marshal — Tidewalker

The Ration Compact

Stance: The cautious middle: freeze new tower construction, ration Hopelight to essential infrastructure, buy time rather than gamble on a fix. Popular with working-class districts who'd bear the brunt of either a mining collapse or an economic boom-bust.

Hook: Coris's private ledger is the single most accurate piece of intelligence in the entire senate. She'd share it with outsiders before she'd share it with most of her colleagues.

Thess Aurelin

Windcaller Senator — Stormling

The Stormcall Expansionists

Stance: Wants to send expeditions beyond the Shining Everlast's borders to locate and claim new ley convergence points elsewhere in Mirrorbloom, founding new tower-colonies.

Hook: Finding out what happened to the missing expedition — and what or who they actually encountered out there — is an open adventure hook with real stakes for the whole senate.

Mira Glasswright

Senator — Glasswright

The Glasswright Compact

Stance: Pushes for radical public transparency about how dire the crisis actually is, rather than the senate's habit of quiet reassurance. Doesn't have a fix — believes the gridlock is partly caused by nobody being honest about how bad things have gotten.

Hook: Mira physically cannot lie to a party that asks her a direct question. She's an extremely efficient source of ground truth, if anyone bothers to ask her the right questions.

The corrupted mirror of the Shining Everlast — towers that were built to reach the sky, now sinking slowly back into the dark.

Overview

Where the Shining Everlast's towers reach upward, the Last Shine's identical towers are folding inward — not collapsing all at once, but compacting, floor by floor, year by year, in a slow, structural implosion that has become simply a fact of life for the fifty million mirrored souls who live there. The same ten major species populate the Last Shine, mirrored and corrupted, and the same vast urban sprawl extends in every direction with no countryside, no horizon, nowhere that isn't city.

The Last Shine runs on Hollowlight: crystal fed not by scarcity but by overflow — the Law of Reflection's spillover from everything genuinely good that happens in the Shining Everlast above. Hollowlight is not scarce. It is abundant, and growing more so every year the city above keeps hoping. The tragedy of the Last Shine is not a shortage of light; it is that five ruthless factions have turned its abundance into an active, low-grade civil war over who gets to control it, leaving ordinary citizens to suffer rolling blackouts caused not by scarcity but by sabotage.

Government

The Last Shine maintains the same Senate structure as its bright twin — species-allocated seats, five major factions, a presiding Speaker — but where the Shining Everlast's gridlock comes from genuine, good-faith disagreement, the Last Shine's factions actively sabotage one another, and its presiding Speaker has no interest whatsoever in resolving anything that pays him so well to remain unresolved.

Geography — Eight Vertical Zones

The Drowned Levels
Below street level (mirrors the Underlevels)

What were once canals have become flooded, half-collapsed tunnels, with Hollowlight conduits running through standing black water rather than clean channels. Tidewalker-mirrored inhabitants here live closest to the city's actual structural failure, and are the first to feel it when a Hoard Court sabotage cuts power to an entire tier.

The Starved Market
Street level (mirrors the Ground Bazaar)

The same dense, multi-species marketplace footprint as its bright counterpart, now picked over and tense — goods are plentiful in theory, since Hollowlight is abundant, but supply lines are constantly disrupted by factional sabotage, leaving genuine scarcity in the middle of genuine abundance.

The Grinding Tiers
Lower-mid towers (mirrors the Foundry Tiers)

Industrial floors run not by proud guild lineage but by the Hoard Court's private militia enforcers, who treat the refineries as a resource to be controlled rather than a craft to be practiced. Workers here have no equivalent of the Deepwrights' genuine, if anxious, pride.

The Rootrot Gardens
Mid towers (mirrors the Greenbelt Spires)

What were vertical gardens above are now the Rootrot Assembly's territory — still green, still tended, but cultivating engineered grief and staged tragedy rather than festivals. The gardens are, by some accounts, still genuinely beautiful, which several visitors have found more disturbing than outright decay would be.

The Gaslight Quarter
Mid-upper towers (mirrors the Lamplight Quarter)

Nightlife here never stopped, but it curdled — a permanent after-hours economy run less by Duskborn pride and more by desperation, since the Starvation Guild's rationing hits the middle tiers hardest and night work has become one of the few reliable ways to get by.

The Cracked Courts
Upper towers (mirrors the Glass Courts)

The Last Shine's Senate chamber, its translucent Glasswright-mirrored architecture visibly fractured in places that have never been repaired — a standing, literal monument to a government that has stopped trying to fix anything, including its own building.

The Fallreach
Highest inhabited towers (mirrors the Stormreach)

The Expansion Cult's stronghold, home to Brexx Talonreach's raiders and the launch point for their border incursions. Genuinely the most dangerous tier in the Last Shine to live in — not from structural risk alone, but from the raiders themselves.

The Sunken Crown
The literal tower-tops, now sinking (mirrors the Crownspires)

The corrupted mirror of the Crownspires, where the oldest towers' peaks are slowly, visibly compacting downward rather than reaching up. The very few who still live here are the city's last true believers in something resembling its bright counterpart's faith — the Last Honest Voice's quiet stronghold.

The Senate

Vantrel Oss

Presiding Chair — Human (mirrors Toravin Hale)

Neutral Chair (in name only)

Stance: Holds the same procedurally neutral chair as his counterpart above — and uses it for the opposite purpose. Vantrel has no interest in resolving the Hollowlight conflict, because the conflict is the entire source of his relevance and income.

Hook: A party that exposes Vantrel's actual position — that he serves no faction but himself — could collapse the deadlock overnight, or could make all five factions briefly, dangerously unified against the party instead.

Hesk Varrin

Conduit-Lord — Emberforged (mirrors Voss Kael)

The Hoard Court

Stance: Controls physical access to the deepest Hollowlight veins through a private militia of guild enforcers. Doesn't want to grow supply — supply is already abundant. Wants to be the only one who can turn the taps on or off.

Hook: Proof of Hesk's sabotage campaigns would be enough to turn even the other four factions against him — if anyone could get it without him finding out first.

Prella Ashcourt

Tier-Warden — Elf

The Starvation Guild

Stance: Believes Hollowlight should be hoarded by the upper towers and old families, rationing the underlevels into literal darkness. Frames this openly as deserved hierarchy rather than necessity.

Hook: Proof of Prella's origins, if surfaced publicly, would devastate her standing with the old families she's spent a lifetime impressing — and might, just possibly, crack something open in her that she's buried for decades.

Moldreth Wane

First-Rot — Rootkin (mirrors Ilyenne Wovenbark)

The Rootrot Assembly

Stance: The dark mirror of the Verdant Accord: instead of cultivating genuine hope to grow more crystal, deliberately manufactures despair — staged tragedies, engineered grief, orchestrated loss — to farm Hollowlight faster.

Hook: Uncovering and stopping Moldreth's long-game plot could be a campaign-spanning thread in its own right — the kind of slow-burn horror that rewards a party paying close attention over many sessions.

Brexx Talonreach

Raid-Caller — Stormling (mirrors Thess Aurelin)

The Expansion Cult

Stance: Mirrors the Stormcall Expansionists' ambition without any of the idealism — openly, violently colonial, raiding neighboring territory for resources rather than negotiating or exploring in good faith.

Hook: Whatever Brexx's raiders found could be the seed of a much larger regional plot — possibly tied to another empire's mirror-territory entirely.

Grael Vess

Senator — Glasswright (mirrors Mira Glasswright)

The Last Honest Voice

Stance: The one faction that isn't purely venal: a small, mostly powerless bloc who believe the Last Shine cannot be saved, and that the only moral act left is making the collapse as humane as possible.

Hook: Grael may be the only senator in the Last Shine an outside party can actually trust at face value — and possibly the only one who would genuinely welcome outside help, if he believed it was real.
